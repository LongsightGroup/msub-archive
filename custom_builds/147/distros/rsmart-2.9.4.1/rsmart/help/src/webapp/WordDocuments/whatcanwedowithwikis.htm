What Can We Do with Wikis?

Wikis were originally designed to help researchers and lecturers collaborate on documents, share information, and create teaching materials. Today, groups of instructors often use wikis as repositories for agendas and lecture or meeting notes, for compiling reading lists and course curricula, and for brainstorming and planning.

In the classroom, wikis are commonly used for group projects. Many such projects formerly relied on MS Word and its Track Changes feature. The participants emailed revised versions to each other and waited for their turn to revise the material under development. Using a wiki for these types of projects eliminates the need to email revisions and allows multiple participants to work on the project simultaneously.

Anecdotal evidence indicates that the use of wikis in the classroom encourages writing, close reading, editing, and student involvement in group activities. The use of wikis also promotes pride of authorship and eases students into the process of writing for an audience. Students can collaborate on assignments by compiling data or ideas they have gathered individually and creating plans and reports as a group. Throughout the process, the instructor can track who has contributed what to the developing material and can track the evolution of student thinking. Perhaps more importantly, the instructor has more points at which to assess and offer guidance than are available in a traditional product-oriented situation.

Other common uses of wikis in the classroom include collaboratively compiling a glossary for a research topic or for content across a course, providing a place where students can contribute helpful advice or links for use by other students, and providing a space to compile outstanding examples of work

Wikis are being used outside of the classroom as well. For example, at some institutions students collaborate to create a student-centered view of their institution.
