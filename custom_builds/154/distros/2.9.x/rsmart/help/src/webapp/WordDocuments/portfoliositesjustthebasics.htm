Portfolio Sites: Just the Basics

Portfolio sites may emphasize activities for the assessment of learning outcomes by linking standards to expectations for work, requiring participants to reflect upon how their work exemplifies relevant standards, collecting reviewer evaluation of work in relation to standards, and reporting on the resulting evaluation in relation to participant work, reflection, and relevant standards.

Portfolio sites may also emphasize participant development by guiding the process of collecting and reflecting upon evidence of learning with the eventual goal of assembling and publishing it for others to review. Development activities may focus on the building of self-esteem, enhancing the connection between participants and a program or institution, and the promotion of meta-cognitive skills in relation to learning.

Examples of portfolio sites include a site for first year students to document and reflect upon activities related to institutional values; a site for Education majors to assemble teaching portfolios in order to look for jobs; a site to assess the outcomes of an undergraduate leadership program; and a site for faculty to use in submitting promotion and tenure files.

Each portfolio site is owned by a user who is given a role (such as Organizer) that allows him or her to add site participants, create a variety of portfolio structures, establish communication with participants, and more. The site owner can also specify who can access and modify the portfolio site: Portfolio sites can be open to the public, or access can be limited to a specific group.

 

If you are a site owner, you must consider a number of different factors before setting up your site. You may want to start by asking yourself these questions:

•      Who are the participants in the site and what are their purposes in creating portfolios?

•      As a site organizer, what do I want site participants to get out of the process of creating a portfolio?

•      What different types of portfolios will site participants create?

•      What level and types of reflection do I want site participants to engage in while producing their portfolios?

•      Who is the intended audience for the portfolios to be produced?

•      Are there external standards that site participants’ portfolios must address?

•      What forms of feedback and/or evaluation need to be incorporated into the process?

•      How will site participants interact with each other on the site?

•      How does the creation of portfolios fit into other activities (course, project, program, co-curricular, career development, job search) in which site participants may be involved?

Depending upon your answers to questions such as these, you may wish to focus on portfolios as product. If this is the case, you may want to limit the tools on your site to support only the basics of collection and publication of students’ work. Alternatively, you may wish to use your site to encourage students to reflect on relationships among different learning objects, content, and disciplines and to think about the process as well as the product of portfolio construction. In this case, you will want to use a broader array of tools.
